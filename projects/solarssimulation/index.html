1. Inductor Design (L)

Objective: Limit current ripple (ΔIL) to ensure continuous conduction.

Switch ON: Inductor stores energy. VL = Vin.

ΔIL = (Vin · D) / (L · fs)

Objective: Limit output voltage ripple (ΔVout) to 1-2%.

Switch ON: Capacitor discharges to load. ΔQ = Iout · D · T.

ΔVout = (Iout · D) / (C · fs)

Solving for C ensures a steady DC output voltage.

System Elements & Design Aims

Power Generation & Conversion

  • Solar PV Array Block: Aim: To simulate real-world solar panel behavior. Generates variable DC power based on irradiance (1000 W/m¬≤) and temperature (25¬∞C) inputs.
  • Boost Converter (L, Switch, Diode, C): Aim: To step up the low PV voltage to a higher target level. Steps up voltage from ~45V to 150V. Inductor stores energy, switch controls flow, diode prevents backflow, capacitor filters ripple.
  • Resistive Load: Aim: To consume the generated power. Represents the application consuming the power (calculated for 1kW at 150V).

Control & Measurement

  • V & I Measurement: Aim: To provide feedback for control logic. Sensors measuring real-time voltage and current for Power calculation (P = V*I).
  • MPPT (P&O) Logic: Aim: To extract maximum available power. Perturb & Observe algorithm using math blocks and delays to track the Maximum Power Point (MPP).
  • PID Controller: Aim: To ensure stability and fast response. Tuned controller (Kp=181.48, Ti=0.0576) that adjusts the duty cycle to maintain stable 150V output with minimal error.
  • PWM Generator: Aim: To drive the electronic switch. Converts the control signal into ON/OFF pulses for the IGBT/MOSFET switch.

Project Journey & Learning Outcomes

Reflections from our Second Year Simulation Project

üìö

Phase 1: Concepts Mastered

Kya Padhe (What We Learned)

The foundational phase of our project was dedicated to understanding the core theoretical concepts that govern renewable energy systems. We delved deep into power electronics, specifically focusing on the Boost Converter Topology. We learned how an inductor, switch, diode, and capacitor can be configured to step up a lower DC voltage to a higher one, and the critical importance of operating in Continuous Conduction Mode (CCM) for stability.

A significant portion of our study was devoted to Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT). We studied the non-linear I-V and P-V characteristics of solar cells and understood why a direct connection to a load is inefficient. We learned various MPPT algorithms, ultimately selecting the "Perturb & Observe" (P&O) method for its balance of simplicity and effectiveness.

Finally, we studied classical Control Systems theory to understand how to tame the dynamic behavior of the converter. We learned about feedback loops, error signals, and how a Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller can be used to eliminate steady-state error and improve transient response.

Phase 2: Skills Acquired

Kya Sikhe (Practical Skills)

Translating theory into practice was the most rewarding part of the journey. Our primary tool was MATLAB & Simulink. We gained proficiency in its library, learning to build complex power systems block-by-block, from PV arrays and IGBTs to control logic gates and PWM generators.

A crucial practical skill was Component Sizing. We didn't just use arbitrary values; we applied the design formulas we derived to calculate the precise Inductance (L) and Capacitance (C) needed to meet our ripple requirements (<1%). This connected the math directly to the simulation performance.

We also learned the art of PID Tuning. Initially attempting manual tuning, we quickly realized its difficulty. We then learned to use MATLAB's automated PID Tuner App. This involved linearizing the plant model at an operating point, identifying its structure (underdamped pair), and interactively adjusting response time and transient behavior to achieve the perfect control loop.

Phase 3: Challenges Faced

Kya Problems Aaye (Hurdles)

üî¥ Problem 1: Open-Loop Instability

Our initial simulations without a controller were highly unstable. The output voltage would fluctuate wildly with any change in input irradiance or load, never settling at the desired 150V.

üü¢ Solution: Implemented a closed-loop PID controller to provide corrective feedback.
üî¥ Problem 2: High Voltage Ripple

Using standard capacitor values, our output voltage had a ripple of over 5%, which is unacceptable for a stable DC supply.

üü¢ Solution: Re-derived the capacitor design formula and calculated the exact value needed for <1% ripple.
üî¥ Problem 3: PID Tuning Difficulty

Manually adjusting the Kp, Ki, and Kd gains was a frustrating process of trial and error. Increasing one gain to improve rise time often led to massive overshoot or oscillations.

üü¢ Solution: Leveraged MATLAB's automated PID Tuner tool to scientifically identify the plant and optimize the gains for a robust response.

Literature Review

PAPER 1 Porselvi et al.

Perturb And Observation Based MPPT Control

Established the need to treat the PV array and converter as a single coupled system. Validated P&O algorithm for maximum power extraction under non-ideal conditions.

PAPER 2 Patel et al.

Simulation of Standalone PV System

Provided mathematical models for PV cells and highlighted the utility of high-gain boost converters for standalone systems. Validated using a 60W Solarex panel.

PAPER 3 Vaikundaselvan et al.

Design of MPPT Based Buck-Boost Converter

Demonstrated that P&O algorithms effectively track MPP despite temperature and irradiance variations. Simulation proved stable power output capability.

PAPER 4 Gupta et al.

Performance Analysis using MATLAB Simulink

Detailed the I-V characteristics logic and validated the MATLAB simulation approach for combining MPPT logic with boost converter topology.
